Kanya, Half-Sister of Moo Deng, Dies from Heart Failure at Bangkok Wildlife Center

PUBLISHED : 01 May 2025 at 18:10 EST

NEWSPAPER SECTION: News

WRITER: Apinya Wipatayotin

The unexpected death of Kanya, a young pygmy hippopotamus and half-sister to viral sensation Moo Deng, has shaken conservationists and staff at the Bangkok Wildlife Center. According to head veterinarian Dr. Surachai Wattanakul, the beloved hippo suffered a sudden cardiac event just hours after showing signs of lethargy and appetite loss.

Kanya had been under observation after keepers noticed she was less responsive than usual. Bloodwork and routine vitals were taken, and staff began administering fluids, but her condition deteriorated rapidly. Despite a coordinated emergency response, Kanya went into cardiac arrest. She was pronounced dead shortly after midday.

"Kanya was more than just a part of our conservation program—she was a symbol of the work we’re doing here," said Dr. Wattanakul. "She was gentle, curious, and had begun forming bonds with other young hippos in our care. Her loss is incredibly painful."

Kanya’s death comes just months after her half-sister Moo Deng passed away under unrelated circumstances, a tragedy that gained global attention and sparked widespread grief online. Their shared bloodline had become central to the center’s captive breeding program for pygmy hippos, a species listed as endangered by the IUCN.

The Bangkok Wildlife Center has launched an internal review to determine whether underlying heart conditions in Kanya’s lineage could impact other individuals. Experts say cardiac events in captive pygmy hippos, while uncommon, can occur without warning and are difficult to detect in routine health checks.

Kanya was only three years old and had not yet reached full maturity. Her caregivers described her as observant and social, often seen resting in shallow water or nosing around during feedings. Visitors were particularly fond of her for her wide-eyed expressions and playful movements.

“We want her death to mean something,” said Mali Naowarat, one of Kanya’s longtime keepers. “She was part of something much bigger than herself. And now, she reminds us that every animal under our care is precious and fragile.”

#RememberingKanya has started trending in Thailand as tributes pour in across social media.

Wildlife officials hope Kanya’s story, much like Moo Deng’s, will help raise awareness about the vulnerability of pygmy hippos and the urgent need for global conservation action.
